TOK Year 1 (Grade 11) · Lesson plan Anchor: "Knowledge and technology" optional theme · placement: early Year 1, alongside truth / certainty / ways of knowing
Unit / Anchor text
Knowledge and technology: Is AI a way of knowing?
Objective SWBAT…
Scholars will be able to define AI in their own words, distinguish rule-based automation, narrow AI, and general AI, and explain why an AI system can sound certain yet be wrong.
Why It Matters
AI now shapes much of what we read, watch, and believe. TOK is about judging knowledge claims, so a knower has to understand where AI knowledge comes from and how far to trust it.
IB Framework
Conceptual / Knowledge Question: To what extent can we trust knowledge produced by AI?
Learner Profile: Thinkers · Knowledgeable
ATLs: Thinking (critical) · Research · Communication
Key Points
  • AI does tasks we once thought needed human intelligence.
  • Almost all AI today is narrow; general AI does not yet exist.
  • Generative AI predicts likely text; it does not "know" or verify.
  • Confident wording is not evidence of truth.
Central Vocabulary / Command Term
Command terms: define · distinguish · evaluate. AI AI terms: automation · narrow AI · general AI · generative AI · hallucination.
Potential Misunderstandings
  • "AI understands what it says." It predicts patterns; it does not understand.
  • "If it sounds confident, it is correct." Fluency is not reliability.
Do First · Do Now 10 min
ROUTINE The Year 1 "I know…" opener: scholars write knowledge claims they hold to be true. AI One claim on the board is an answer copied from an AI chatbot. Is it true? How would you check?
Introduction to New Material & Teacher Model 20 min · I do
AI Concept Video 1 ("Automation, narrow AI, and generative AI", ~88s). The teacher models judging one AI answer against the partner's own truth criteria: does it correspond to the facts, is it coherent, does it work in practice?
Guided Practice 15 min · we do
ROUTINE Their "refute or confirm the claim" structure. AI In two teams, scholars evaluate three AI outputs and decide AI or not, reliable or not, using the three truth criteria.
Independent Practice 10 min · you do
AI In pairs, scholars draft one TOK-style knowledge question about AI that could become an Exhibition prompt.
Formative Assessment / Exit Ticket
Classify three everyday tools as automation, narrow AI, or general AI, justify one; and write one knowledge question about trusting AI. Exemplar in the Teacher Guide.
Feeds the IB-graded pieces IB
Exhibition: the drafted knowledge question sits beside the real IA prompt "What counts as good evidence for a claim?" Essay: seeds reliability-of-knowledge arguments. Scaffolds toward both; replaces neither.

What each of the 17 elements IS, what the expert provides, and what the design team builds. This is for everyone, the partner and the expert alike, so we share one definition of what the map asks for. Examples are from a foundations lesson; the elements are the same for every grade.

Reading-level rule: scholar-facing prose at grade 7 to 9 readability; every video captioned; academic vocabulary taught with cognates for emergent bilingual scholars; nothing depends on sound or idioms.

Foundation

1 · Learning outcome one observable sentence

What a scholar can do after the lesson, in observable verbs; never "understand" or "know." The expert names the capability; the design team phrases it and aligns the assessment.

2 · Key concepts the 3 to 5 ideas that must survive

If a scholar remembers only these, the lesson worked. Each maps to an assessment item and usually a video. The expert provides the concepts and the misconceptions that fight them. In TOK these become a knowledge question.

3 · Vocabulary, Beck's three tiers EB instruction standard

Tier 1 everyday words · Tier 2 high-utility academic words (the sweet spot for EB scholars, with cognates) · Tier 3 domain terms. Focus on Tiers 2 and 3. In TOK, AI terms sit beside the IB command terms.

4 · Standards mapping one code per lesson

The standard the lesson satisfies, cited by code. For TOK the alignment is to the existing TOK course (TEKS being confirmed). Design-team task; the expert provides nothing.

Scholar materials

5 · Student-facing content the lesson body, in chunks

Hook → concept (with video) → guided try → independent try → close. Concrete before abstract; a real-world hook every time. The expert supplies substance and a worked example; AI drafts to the audience; the expert signs off.

6 · Concept videos ×2 60 to 90 sec, one idea each

One short captioned video per key-concept cluster: one idea, one analogy. The videos carry the teaching so the teacher need not be an AI expert.

7 · Scenario video 90 to 120 sec, a real moment

The idea working or failing in a real moment: near-peer character, concrete task, consequence. In TOK it also seeds a knowledge question.

8 · In-class activity do or judge, not watch

Hands-on practice during the period: stump it · catch it · fix it · judge it. The expert says what practice should look like and what failure looks like.

9 · Going-deeper segment one step past the intro

The layer that keeps the period rich. In TOK this is the knowledge-question debate that feeds the Essay and Exhibition. The expert provides the one insight they wish scholars knew.

10 · Extension activity optional, after class

A deeper optional task for scholars who want more; differentiates without depending on homework.

Teacher enablement

11 · Teacher Guide template, 11 sections

Makes the lesson teachable by a non-expert: the one idea, why it matters, prep, run of show, teacher moves, misconceptions, differentiation, materials, assessment + key, connection, open items. The expert provides the misconceptions, the why, and the pitfalls.

12 · Teacher video 2 to 3 min

The guide's elevator version: why it matters, the one idea to protect, the moves to lean on, the activity moment, the misconception to watch, how to read the exit ticket. Watched once before teaching.

Assessment, supports & future

13 · Formative assessment exit ticket

Built to auto-grade where possible; every item maps to a key concept. The expert provides one assessment idea and the misconceptions that become distractors.

14 · Answer key / rubric scoring

Scoring for every item and which misses signal which reteach. Lives in the Teacher Guide.

15 · Pre-assessment / baseline pre/post data

Short baseline before the unit; items mirror the formative banks. Runs in the partner's systems; the university receives de-identified aggregates.

16 · Discussion scaffolds talking points + sentence stems

Teacher talking points + scholar sentence stems, bilingual-friendly, using Tier 2 vocabulary so academic language gets rehearsed.

17 · AI-tutor knowledge base RAG, internal

The content distilled into retrievable chunks, embedded so the future tutor answers grounded in approved content. The expert checks accuracy. Internal until the tutor ships.

The proposed 11th-grade curriculum (TOK): 4 units, 16 lessons, one semester, the first half of a two-year TOK sequence. Lessons run about 45 minutes; the AI segment runs about 3+ weeks. This is a projected draft for your feedback; 11th and 12th grade do not run in units the way 10th does, so the final shape may change. Click a lesson to expand.

Unit 1 · Foundations of AI

Lessons 1 to 4
1 · What Is AI, Really?
EQ: What separates intelligence from automation? Flow: hook game (10) · definitions lecture (15) · discussion (10) · exit ticket (10).
2 · How Computers "Think" + ML Basics
EQ: How do machines learn from data? Flow: pattern demo (10) · mini-lecture (15) · dataset activity (15) · reflection (5).
3 · Neural Networks & Deep Learning, Simplified
EQ: How do layered networks "learn"? Flow: visual demo (10) · mini-lecture (20) · concept-mapping (15).
4 · Generative AI Decoded
EQ: How does AI create new content? Flow: GenAI walkthrough (15) · hands-on (20) · discussion (10).

Unit 2 · Using AI Effectively & Responsibly

Lessons 5 to 8
5 · Prompt Engineering 101 & 201
EQ: How does how we ask change the output? Flow: demo (10) · guided prompt lab (25) · challenge + share (10).
6 · When AI Gets It Wrong: Hallucinations & Fact-Checking
EQ: How do we catch AI mistakes? Flow: spot-the-error (15) · mini-lecture (15) · fact-check lab (15).
7 · Bias in AI
EQ: Where does AI bias come from? Flow: case examples (15) · mini-lecture (15) · discussion (15).
8 · Your Data, Their Model: Privacy
EQ: What happens to the data we give AI? Flow: data-trail activity (15) · mini-lecture (15) · checklist creation (15).

Unit 3 · AI Ethics & Society

Lessons 9 to 12
9 · Who's Responsible? Accountability & Decision-Making
EQ: Who is accountable when AI decides? Flow: scenario debate (15) · framework lecture (15) · group analysis (15).
10 · Academic Integrity + Deepfakes & Synthetic Media
EQ: Can we trust what we see, and use AI honestly? Flow: integrity discussion (15) · deepfake detection (15) · reflection (15).
11 · Who Owns AI-Generated Work?
EQ: Who is the author of AI output? Flow: mini-lecture (15) · case debate (20) · position write-up (10).
12 · Case Study Lab: AI Dilemmas in the Real World
EQ: How do we reason through real AI dilemmas? Flow: case lab (35) · group findings share (10).

Unit 4 · AI Across Industries & Applied Skills

Lessons 13 to 16
13 · AI Across Industries: Healthcare, Business & Creative
EQ: How is AI reshaping major fields? Flow: examples (15) · mini-lecture (20) · discussion (10).
14 · AI in Science & the Future of Work
EQ: How is AI changing discovery and careers? Flow: discovery case (15) · mini-lecture (15) · skills mapping (15).
15 · No-Code AI Build + Choosing the Right Tool
EQ: How do I build with and select AI tools? Flow: build walkthrough (20) · hands-on build (20) · tool comparison (5).
16 · Designing Responsible AI + The Road Ahead
EQ: How do we shape a responsible AI future? Flow: principles (15) · design activity (20) · reflection (10).
