Statement of Inquiry
Emerging technologies influence communication and innovation.
Conceptual Understanding
Technology influences how individuals communicate, access information, and make decisions, while perspectives shape innovation and problem-solving.
Global Context
Scientific and Technical Innovation: processes and solutions.
Key & Related Concepts
Key: Communication. Related: Function.
Inquiry Questions
Factual: What is AI? What is bias in AI? What data trains AI? Conceptual: How does bias impact AI-generated information? How does understanding AI support ethical decisions? Debatable: To what extent does AI enhance or limit human thinking?
ATL skills
Research, Information Literacy: evaluate AI-generated content for accuracy and bias; use digital tools responsibly and ethically.
Summative (GRASPS)
AI Industry Solution Proposal. As an AI Ethics Consultant, investigate a persistent AI problem in one industry and design a practical solution (ethical guidelines, a verification checklist, a redesigned workflow, or a human-plus-AI model). Scored on MYP Design Criteria A to D.

One daily lesson inside the unit (Lesson 1):

Content Flow

Lesson 1

TEKS: §127.19(d)(1), investigate a problem and define the project. Generic / research-aligned; confirm local coding.

Inquiry & Analysis

Teaching/Learning Objectives

TWBAT introduce a working definition of AI and distinguish it from automation, machine learning, and general AI.

SWBAT define AI, ML, and automation; distinguish narrow vs. general AI; and identify AI in daily life, justifying each.

Resources

  • U1L1 slide deck
  • "AI or Not?" sort cards (about 10 tools)
  • Concept Video 1: "Automation, narrow AI, and general AI" (~88s, captioned)
  • Concept Video 2: "From rules to learning" (~80s, captioned)
  • Scholar handout · exit ticket · MYP Design Journal
  • Class-approved AI tool [confirm]

Instructional Note: Protect the one idea: almost all AI today is narrow, and AI is not one single thing; the goal is to classify it. Keep it concrete; no neural-network math. The "AI or Not?" sort is where the idea lands, and it seeds Lesson 2 (rules vs. learning). Full run of show, misconceptions, and EB/SPED differentiation in the Teacher Guide.

Key Points

Inquiry & Analysis

Essential Question: What separates true intelligence from automation?

Lesson (90-minute run of show, the approved curriculum flow):

0-15 Hook, "AI or Not?" sorting game: everyday tools on screen (calculator, thermostat, a "for you" row, a self-driving clip, spellcheck, a chatbot). Scholars vote AI or not, in complete sentences. Surface the disagreement; that is the hook.

15-40 Mini-lecture on definitions: AI vs. machine learning vs. automation; narrow vs. general AI; why almost all AI today is narrow; the common myths.

40-45 Checkpoint (quick write, Design Journal): define AI in your own words and classify one tool you use, and why. ← 45-minute campuses split here; Session 1 ends on this checkpoint.

45-60 Pair discussion + real-world examples: in pairs, scholars name AI they use daily and say which type and why; share two with the class.

60-80 Group sort activity: classify the full set of about ten tools as automation, narrow AI, or general AI, and justify two in writing. Build the class chart; surface the borderline cases.

80-90 Exit ticket (Cell 4). Session 2 (45-min campuses) runs the pair discussion through the exit ticket.

Assessment / Product / Checkpoint

Checkpoint / Exit ticket: Define AI in your own words and give one real example; then say whether one tool you use is automation, narrow AI, or general AI, and why.

Assessment: MYP Design Criterion A (Inquiring and Analyzing).

  • analyze a range of existing products that inspire a solution
  • explain and justify the need for a solution for a specified audience

Exit ticket (5 items, 7 points; full version + key in the Teacher Guide): classify four tools and justify one; "most AI today is narrow" (MCQ); automation follows rules while ML finds patterns (MCQ); define AI with one example; spot the myth (MCQ).

Foundation

1 · Learning outcome one observable sentence

What a scholar can do after the lesson, in observable verbs (explain, identify, evaluate, produce); never "understand" or "know." The expert names the capability; the design team phrases it and aligns the assessment.

Lesson 1: Scholars can define AI in their own words and classify everyday technologies as automation, narrow AI, or general AI, justifying each.
2 · Key concepts the 3 to 5 ideas that must survive

If a scholar remembers only these, the lesson worked. Each maps to an assessment item and usually a video. The expert provides the concepts and the misconceptions that fight them.

Lesson 1: AI does tasks we thought needed human intelligence · automation vs. narrow vs. general AI · most AI today is narrow · rules vs. learning from data.
3 · Vocabulary, Beck's three tiers EB instruction standard

Organized by Beck's tiered model: Tier 1 everyday words (gloss for newcomers) · Tier 2 high-utility academic words (predict, evaluate, verify, bias), the sweet spot for EB scholars, taught with Spanish cognates where they exist · Tier 3 domain terms (generative AI, LLM, training data, prompt). Focus on Tiers 2 and 3.

Lesson 1: Tier 2 = classify, distinguish, justify, define · Tier 3 = automation, narrow AI, general AI, machine learning.
4 · TEKS mapping one code per lesson

The TEKS strand the lesson satisfies, cited by code. Host course 19 TAC §127.19 (CTE Project-Based Capstone). The partner confirms the local coding. Design-team task; the expert provides nothing.

Lesson 1: §127.19(d)(1).

Scholar materials

5 · Student-facing content the lesson body, in 10 to 12 min chunks

Structure: hook → concept (with video) → guided try → independent try → close. Never one long read; concrete before abstract; a real-world hook every time; grade 7 to 9 readability. The expert supplies substance and a worked example; AI drafts to the audience; the expert signs off.

6 · Concept videos ×2 60 to 90 sec, one idea each

One short captioned video per key-concept cluster: one idea, one analogy, ends restating the idea. The videos carry the teaching so the teacher need not be an AI expert.

Lesson 1: "Automation, narrow AI, and general AI" · "From rules to learning."
7 · Scenario video 90 to 120 sec, the idea in a real field

The core idea working or failing in a real moment of the scholars' pathway industry: near-peer character, concrete task, consequence. Answers "why does this matter to me."

Lesson 1: someone trusts a voice assistant, asks it something outside its job, gets a confident wrong answer, and realizes it is narrow AI.
8 · In-class activity do or judge, not watch

Hands-on practice during the period. House styles: stump it · catch it · fix it · judge it. The expert says what practice should look like and what failure looks like.

Lesson 1: the "AI or Not?" sort, classify about ten tools and justify two.
9 · Going-deeper segment one step past the intro

The layer that keeps the period rich and strong scholars engaged. The expert provides the one "most people don't know this" insight. Carries the discussion scaffolds (element 16).

Lesson 1: almost all AI today is narrow; deep learning is what made it capable; one true failure case.
10 · Extension activity optional, after class

A deeper optional task for scholars who want more; differentiates without depending on homework.

Lesson 1: "AI in my field", one real AI use in your pathway, its type, one limit, three sentences.

Teacher enablement

11 · Teacher Guide template, 11 sections

Makes the lesson teachable by a non-expert: the one idea that must stick · why it matters · prep checklist · run of show (90 and 45) · teacher moves · misconceptions (claim → correction → how to surface) · differentiation · materials · assessment + key · capstone connection · open items. The expert provides the misconceptions, the why, and the pitfalls.

12 · Teacher video 2 to 3 min, the guide's elevator version

Why it matters → the one idea to protect → the moves to lean on → the activity moment that lands → the misconception to watch → how to read the exit ticket. Watched once before teaching; never shown in class.

Assessment & data

13 · Formative assessment exit ticket, 5 to 7 items

Built to auto-grade where possible: 2 to 3 MCQs whose distractors are the real misconceptions · 1 classification · 1 scenario judgment · 1 short response. Every item maps to a key concept. The expert provides one assessment idea and the misconceptions that become distractors.

Lesson 1: the 5-item / 7-point exit ticket.
14 · Answer key / rubric scoring + how to read results

Scoring for every item, what 1-point vs 2-point responses look like, and which misses signal which reteach. Lives in the Teacher Guide.

15 · Pre-assessment / baseline pre/post data

Short baseline before the unit; items mirror the formative banks so pre/post is comparable. Runs in the partner's systems so identifiable data never leaves their environment; the university receives de-identified aggregates.

Supports & future

16 · Discussion scaffolds talking points + sentence stems

Half a page per discussion: teacher talking points + scholar sentence stems, bilingual-friendly (claim, evidence, counter, connection). Stems use Tier 2 vocabulary so academic language gets rehearsed.

17 · AI-tutor knowledge base RAG foundation, internal

The lesson's content distilled into structured, retrievable chunks (concept summaries, worked examples, misconception → correction pairs), embedded into a vector database so the future AI tutor answers grounded in approved content. The expert checks accuracy; the design team handles the engineering. Internal until the tutor ships.

How these lessons align to Texas standards (TEKS). The host course is the CTE Project-Based Capstone, governed by 19 TAC §127.19. Because §127.19 defines a project process (eight knowledge-and-skills strands), not AI content, each AI lesson maps to the §127.19(d) strand it builds toward and that scholars apply in their capstone project.

The eight §127.19(d) capstone skills (the project process scholars work through; AI content is applied inside it):

  1. Investigate a problem and define the project.
  2. Develop key partnerships (mentors, advisors, collaborators).
  3. Set timeline goals and benchmarks.
  4. Build a project-management timeline for deliverables.
  5. Create a risk analysis (financial, ethical, regulatory, environmental, legal).
  6. Identify and secure necessary approvals.
  7. Implement the project to professional standards.
  8. Present and justify findings to an audience.
