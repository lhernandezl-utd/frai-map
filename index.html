Key Points

Inquiry & Analysis

Essential Questions: What makes something artificial intelligence, and not just a clever program? What are the three kinds (automation, narrow AI, and general AI), and why is almost all AI today narrow?

Lesson: JUN 10 — timestamped

0–5 Do Now: (Design Journal) Six everyday things on screen (calculator, thermostat, a "for you" recommendation row, a self-driving car clip, spellcheck, a chatbot). Scholars vote AI or not, in complete sentences. Surface the disagreement; that is the hook.

5–25 Guided Investigation: Concept Video 1 ("Automation, narrow AI, and AGI"). As a class, classify three of the Do Now items and say why; expect disagreement, that is the point. Then Concept Video 2 ("From rules to learning"): first we wrote the rules, then machines learned patterns from data, which is why AI suddenly got so good.

25–47 Independent Practice: "AI or Not?" sort. In pairs, classify the full set of about ten tools as automation, narrow AI, or AGI, and justify two answers in writing. Build the class chart from what scholars decided, and surface the borderline cases.

47–50 Checkpoint ← 45-minute campuses split here

50–60 Group discussion: Going Deeper — almost all AI today is narrow; what changed recently is deep learning, which made narrow AI suddenly very good; one true failure case. Talking points + sentence stems provided. JUN 10

60–90 Closing: In the Design Journal, scholars name one industry they might investigate for the AI Industry Solution Proposal and one thing they would check about how it uses AI.

Assessment / Product / Checkpoint

Checkpoint: In your own words, define AI and give one real example; then say whether one tool you use is automation, narrow AI, or AGI, and why.

Assessment: MYP Design Criteria A (Inquiring and Analyzing)

  • analyze a range of existing products that inspire a solution to the problem
  • supports: explain and justify the need for a solution to a problem for a specified client/target audience

Exit ticket (5 items, 7 points; full version + answer key in the Teacher Guide): classify four tools as automation, narrow AI, or AGI and justify one; "most AI today is narrow" (MCQ); automation follows written rules while machine learning finds patterns (MCQ); define AI in your own words with one example; spot the myth (MCQ).

The audience everything is written for: 10th grade, ~15 years old, high AI usage / low training, attention in 10–12 minute chunks, mixed reading levels with many emergent bilingual (EB) scholars, Chromebooks, little homework. Reading-level rule: scholar-facing prose drafts at grade 7–9 readability; every video captioned; nothing depends on sound or idioms.

Foundation

1 · Learning outcome — one observable sentence

What a scholar can DO after the module, in observable verbs (explain, identify, evaluate, produce); never "understand" or "know." SME names the capability; design team phrases it and aligns the assessment to it.

Module 1: Scholars can define AI in their own words and classify everyday technologies as rule-based automation, narrow AI, or general AI (AGI), justifying each.
2 · Key concepts — the 3–5 ideas that must survive

If a scholar remembers only these, the module worked. Each maps to at least one assessment item and usually one video. SME provides the concepts and the misconceptions that fight them.

Module 1: AI does tasks we thought needed human intelligence · automation vs. narrow AI vs. general AI (AGI) · most AI today is narrow · rules vs. learning from data.
3 · Vocabulary — Beck's three tiers JUN 10 — EB instruction standard

Organized by Beck, McKeown & Kucan's tiered model: Tier 1 everyday words (gloss/cognate support for newcomers only) · Tier 2 high-utility academic words across domains (predict, evaluate, reliable, verify, bias) — the instructional sweet spot for EB scholars, taught explicitly with Spanish cognates where they exist (predecir, evaluar, verificar) · Tier 3 domain terms (generative AI, LLM, training data, prompt), taught at point of use.

Focus: Tiers 2 and 3. Every term gets a one-sentence scholar-facing definition at grade 7–9 readability.

Module 1: Tier 2 = classify, distinguish, justify, define · Tier 3 = AI, automation, narrow AI, general AI (AGI), machine learning, training data, model/LLM.
4 · TEKS mapping — one code per module

The TEKS strand the module satisfies, cited by code. Host course: 19 TAC §127.19 (CTE Project-Based Capstone). Partner confirms the local coding pattern. Design-team task; SME provides nothing.

Module 1: §127.19(1)(A).

Scholar materials

5 · Student-facing content — the lesson body, in 10–12 min chunks

Structure: hook → concept (with video) → guided try → independent try → close. Never one long read; concrete before abstract; a career/real-world hook every time; grade 7–9 readability. SME supplies substance + a worked example via intake; AI drafts to the audience profile; SME signs off.

6 · Concept videos ×2 — 60–90 sec, one idea each

One short captioned video per key-concept cluster: one idea, one analogy, ends restating the idea. The videos carry the teaching so the teacher need not be an AI expert. Script template: narration + on-screen text + visual direction + length (four-column storyboard).

Module 1: "Automation, narrow AI, and AGI" (~88s) · "From rules to learning" (~80s).
7 · Scenario video — 90–120 sec, the idea in THEIR industry

The core idea working or failing in a real moment of the scholars' pathway industry: near-peer character, concrete task, consequence. Answers "why does this matter to ME." Pathway versions swap the setting, not the concept.

Module 1: Maya trusts her voice assistant, asks it something outside its job, gets a confident wrong answer, and realizes it is narrow AI.
8 · In-class activity — do or catch, not watch

Hands-on practice during the period. House styles: stump it (make the AI fail) · catch it (find the planted error) · fix it (rewrite a weak prompt/output) · judge it (label outputs trustworthy / not / made-up). Pairs on Chromebooks, or facilitator demo when devices are shared. SME says what practice should look like and what failure looks like.

Module 1: "AI or Not?" sort — 15 min, classify about ten tools as automation, narrow AI, or AGI, justify two.
9 · Going-deeper segment — one step past the free intro

The layer that keeps the period rich and strong scholars engaged, at 10th-grade depth. SME provides the one "most people don't know this" insight they wish students knew. Now carries the discussion scaffolds (element 16).

Module 1: almost all AI today is narrow · deep learning is what made it so capable · one true failure case.
10 · Extension activity — optional, after class

A deeper optional task for scholars who want more; differentiates without depending on homework; usually feeds the pathway connection or portfolio.

Module 1: "AI in my field" — one real AI use in your pathway's industry, its type, one limit, 3 sentences.

Teacher enablement

11 · Teacher Guide — template, 11 sections

Makes the lesson teachable by a non-expert. Canonical sections, in order: the one idea that must stick · why this lesson matters · prep checklist · run of show (minute-by-minute, 90-min + 45-min split) · teacher moves that make it land · misconceptions (claim → correction → how to surface) · differentiation (EB/MLL, SPED, extension) · materials · assessment + answer key · capstone connection · open items. SME provides the misconceptions, the "why," and pitfalls.

12 · Teacher video — 2–3 min, the guide's elevator version

Script structure: why this lesson matters (30s) → the one idea to protect (20s) → the analogies/moves to lean on (40s) → the activity moment that makes it land (30s) → the misconception to watch (20s) → how to read the exit ticket (20s). Watched once before teaching; never shown in class.

Video rules JUN 22
  • Budget: about 5 minutes of student video per class; a longer block is filled with more activity, not more video.
  • Count: the three video types are a menu, not a checklist; each lesson uses only what it needs.
  • Length: concept 60–90 sec per idea (about 3–5 min total at most); scenario 90–120 sec.
  • By unit: the foundations unit leans on animated concept explainers (scenario light); the using-AI and ethics units lean on real-case and dilemma clips (scenario central); the applied units use short tool demos.
  • Hybrid: the avatar or motion graphics carry the narration and the diagrams; the SME appears on camera only in short, high-value moments, in their own voice; the scenario is always the near-peer character, never the SME.
  • When: videos are built after the SME intake; we draw the on-camera points from the answers, the SME approves a short suggested brief, then we record.

Assessment & data

13 · Formative assessment — exit ticket, 5–7 items, online-friendly

Item mix built to auto-grade where possible: 2–3 MCQs whose distractors are the real misconceptions · 1 matching/classification · 1 scenario judgment · 1 short constructed response (teacher- or AI-assisted scoring). Every item maps to a key concept; EB scholars may answer open items in their stronger language. SME provides one assessment idea + the misconceptions that become distractors.

Module 1: the 5-item / 7-point exit ticket.
14 · Answer key / rubric — scoring + how to read results

Scoring for every item, what 1-point vs 2-point open responses look like, and which misses signal which reteach. Lives in the Teacher Guide, never in scholar materials.

15 · Pre-assessment / baseline JUN 10 — pre/post data

Short baseline BEFORE the module/unit; items mirror the formative banks so pre/post is directly comparable. Produces the partner's data points and the grant's evaluation evidence.

Administration & data (pending decisions): runs in the partner's systems so identifiable student data never leaves their environment; the university receives de-identified aggregates. Timing: first ~10 min of Lesson 1, or a standalone sitting. Ties to the hosting decision.

Supports & future

16 · Discussion scaffolds JUN 10 — talking points + sentence stems

Half a page per discussion segment: teacher talking points (3–4 moves that keep the discussion alive) + scholar sentence stems, bilingual-friendly. Stem styles: claim ("I think ___ because ___") · evidence ("In the activity I saw ___, which shows ___") · counter ("I see it differently; ___") · connection ("This matters in [industry] because ___"). Stems deliberately use Tier 2 vocabulary so academic language gets rehearsed, not just content.

17 · AI-tutor knowledge base — RAG foundation, internal

The module's content distilled into structured, retrievable chunks: concept summaries, worked examples, misconception → correction pairs. Chunks are embedded into a vector database; when a scholar asks the future AI tutor a question, the system retrieves the relevant chunks and the tutor answers grounded in approved course content (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) instead of improvising. Also the control surface: the tutor only stands on SME-approved content. SME checks accuracy; design team handles chunking, embeddings, retrieval. Internal until the tutor ships.

How an SME uses this: at intake you provide the learning outcome, key concepts + misconceptions, Tier 3 terms + the Tier 2 verbs you lean on, one or two real cases from your field, what practice should look like, your going-deeper layer, and one assessment idea. The design team builds everything else and returns it for your review; nothing ships without your sign-off.

Unit 1 · Foundations: What Is AI, Really?

Builds a ground-up mental model: what AI is, how machines learn, and how generative AI works, before any tool use or ethics discussion.

Lesson 1 · What Is AI, Really? — what makes something AI vs. just a clever program?

Objectives. Teacher: introduce a working definition of AI and distinguish it from automation, machine learning, and AGI. Student: classify everyday technologies as rule-based automation, narrow AI, or (hypothetical) general AI, and justify.

Standards. ISTE Knowledge Constructor (map to local TEKS).

45-minute flow. Video (5): explainer contrasting automation, narrow AI, and sci-fi AI. Lecture (25): history (rule-based → machine learning → deep learning → generative); rules vs. learning from data; narrow vs. general AI; common myths. Exercise (15): "AI or Not?" sort of 10 tools, justify two.

Checkpoint. Exit ticket: define AI in your own words, one example.

90-minute expansion. AI Timeline gallery walk, then a short debate: "Is my smart fridge intelligent?"

Lesson 2 · How Computers "Think": Data, Rules, and Patterns — how is "learning" different from being programmed?

Objectives. Teacher: the difference between rule-based programming and pattern-based learning. Student: trace an algorithm's input → process → output, and identify where fixed rules versus learned patterns appear.

Standards. ISTE Computational Thinker (map to local TEKS).

45-minute flow. Video (5): algorithms and flowcharts. Lecture (25): what an algorithm is (input, process, output); if-then rules vs. statistical patterns; first look at "training data"; where each shows up in daily apps. Exercise (15): build a rule-based flowchart ("should I bring an umbrella?"), then discuss how a learned model would reach it differently.

Checkpoint. Submit the flowchart with one reflection sentence.

90-minute expansion. Unplugged "human classifier" game: sort by an explicit rule, then by pattern alone, compare.

Lesson 3 · Machine Learning Basics: Training, Data, and Prediction — how does a machine learn from examples?

Objectives. Teacher: supervised learning conceptually (training data, labels, prediction). Student: describe the role of training data and identify data-quality issues in a sample dataset.

Standards. ISTE Computational Thinker (map to local TEKS).

45-minute flow. Video (5): supervised learning with labeled images. Lecture (25): training data and labels; train/test split; overfitting (memorizing vs. generalizing); garbage in, garbage out. Exercise (15): "Train the Human" with labeled example cards.

Checkpoint. Three short-answer questions on training data and prediction.

90-minute expansion. Hands-on no-code demo (e.g., Teachable Machine) to train an actual image classifier.

Lesson 4 · Neural Networks & Deep Learning, Simplified — why the brain comparison, and where it breaks down?

Objectives. Teacher: a simplified model (inputs, weights, layers, output). Student: describe in plain language how a network processes input through layers to a decision.

Standards. ISTE Knowledge Constructor (map to local TEKS).

45-minute flow. Video (5): animated neural network explainer. Lecture (25): neurons, weights, layers; why "deep"; where the brain analogy misleads; why networks need data and compute. Exercise (15): paper "mini neural network" weighted-scoring decision.

Checkpoint. Reflection on how changing a weight changed the decision.

90-minute expansion. Live exploration with a visualization tool (e.g., TensorFlow Playground).

Lesson 5 · Generative AI Decoded: How Tools Like ChatGPT Actually Work — if it doesn't "understand", how does it write so well?

Objectives. Teacher: LLMs conceptually (tokens, next-word prediction, training on text). Student: describe what an LLM does when it generates text, and why it can sound confident yet be wrong.

Standards. ISTE Knowledge Constructor (map to local TEKS).

45-minute flow. Video (5): how LLMs predict the next word/token. Lecture (25): tokens and next-word prediction; training on massive text; why outputs feel human is not the same as understanding; other generative AI (image, audio, video). Exercise (15): "Predict the Next Word" game vs. an actual tool.

Checkpoint. Two sentences on how a chatbot picks its next word.

90-minute expansion. Live demo comparing 2 to 3 generative tools on the same prompt.

Unit 2 · Using AI Effectively & Responsibly

Moves from concepts to practice: how to prompt well, and how to spot the failure modes (hallucination, bias, privacy) of everyday use.

Lesson 6 · Prompt Engineering 101: How to Talk to AI — why do two people get different answers?

Objectives. Teacher: foundational prompting (clarity, context, role, format). Student: write a prompt using at least three techniques and compare it to a weaker version.

Standards. ISTE Empowered Learner (map to local TEKS).

45-minute flow. Video (5): practical prompting tips. Lecture (25): anatomy of a good prompt; common mistakes; role prompting; iterative refinement. Exercise (15): prompt-rewrite challenge, test both versions.

Checkpoint. One before/after prompt pair with an explanation.

90-minute expansion. Prompting tournament with peer critique against a rubric.
